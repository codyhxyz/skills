- [ ] **Skill: `instance-vs-invariant`** (rename candidates if the structural framing feels too narrow now: `candidate-vs-canon`, `floated-vs-decided`, `e.g.-vs-i.e.`, `could-vs-should`) — Catch the moment an example, candidate, or prior-turn suggestion gets silently promoted to a spec, norm, or decision. The shared invariant: **don't let "here's an example of what could be" turn into "here's all of what should be"** — and require explicit ratification before any candidate gets promoted to canon. Two trigger surfaces:

    1. **Structural** (lists-in-definitions): when describing a design, strategy, or principle, watch the moment you start enumerating its contents — a list of lenses, a template's bullets, a property triple, a checklist. Pause and ask: am I describing what this thing *is* (the invariant — what must be true for it to count) or what one filling-in of it *could look like* (an instance)? If the latter, mark it explicitly ("e.g.", "one instance would be") rather than letting it sit flat as definitional. Those lists are almost always instances of an underlying invariant, and presenting them as the spec smuggles examples into normativity without the user's consent.

    2. **Temporal** (turn-boundary smuggling, formerly `could-should-smuggling`): a way something *could* be done — especially something floated in a previous conversational turn, a hypothetical, an "e.g." Claude itself proposed, or a brainstorm bullet — gets quietly codified as the way it *should* be done. A contingent suggestion becomes load-bearing precedent, options collapse into mandates, a brainstorm bullet becomes the architecture. Pause and ask: did we *decide* this, or did we just *talk about* it?

    Both triggers share the same fix: mark the candidate as a candidate, and require explicit ratification before promotion. Hume's is/ought guillotine, applied to AI conversations.
