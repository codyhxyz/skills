Regional dialect. Synonyms, antonyms. Speaking in lists, verbal tics. All of these are levers you can and should be pulling. An LLM doing these things for the love of the game. - [ ] **Skill: `instance-vs-invariant`** — When describing a design, strategy, or principle, watch for the moment you start enumerating its contents — a list of lenses, a template's bullets, a property triple, a checklist. At that moment, pause and ask: am I describing what this thing *is* (the invariant — what must be true for it to count), or what one filling-in of it *could look like* (an instance)? If the latter, mark it explicitly ("e.g.", "one instance would be") rather than letting it sit flat as definitional. Trigger to fire on: any numbered or bulleted list appearing inside a definition. Those lists are almost always instances of an underlying invariant, and presenting them as the spec smuggles examples into normativity without the user's consent. Plain-English: don't let "here's an example of what could be" turn into "here's all of what should be."

- [ ] **Skill: `could-should-smuggling`** (working name; better names welcome — `suggestion-as-spec`? `precedent-creep`? `path-to-policy`? `proposal-to-canon`? `is-from-ought-turn-history`?) — AI's tendency to take a way something *could* be done — especially something floated in a previous conversational turn, an example, a "for instance", or a hypothetical Claude itself proposed — and silently codify it as the way something *should* be done. The smuggling happens at turn boundaries: a contingent suggestion becomes load-bearing precedent, options collapse into mandates, a brainstorm bullet becomes the architecture. Trigger to fire on: any moment Claude treats a prior-turn artifact (its own or the user's) as a spec rather than a candidate, especially when no explicit ratification happened. Plain-English: just because we *talked about* doing it that way doesn't mean we *decided* to. Sibling to `instance-vs-invariant` — that one catches the fallacy at the structural/list level; this one catches it across conversational time. Worth deciding whether they're two skills or one skill with two triggers.
